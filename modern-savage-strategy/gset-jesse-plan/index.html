Headline

We're flipping GSET from a drama-production to a single-reader audiobook-immersive series for YouTube. 20 episodes, ~8 minutes each, dropping fortnightly from the anniversary on 19 June 2026.

One studio shoot of 2–3 days produces all visual content for the entire 40-week run. Total year-one cost lands at £18–26k — roughly half of the previously-modelled drama budget — with materially lower execution risk.

What the format actually looks like (Hybrid)

Every episode is built around one chapter of The Greatest Story Ever Told audiobook. The chapter audio carries the narrative; the visual layer is two things, intercut:

  • The spine (60–70%): one professional reader on-camera in a single neutral studio set — sitting, sometimes standing, performing the chapter silently while the audiobook plays beneath. Same person across all 20 episodes — the recognisable visual anchor of the series.
  • The bed (30–40%): atmospheric B-roll cutaways that match the chapter's setting and emotional beats — landscapes, hands, candlelight, scripture text, ambient texture. Cinematic, restrained, no dialogue overlays.

This gives YouTube the human anchor a viewer locks onto, plus the immersive quality that justifies the "immersive audiobook takes" framing. It is materially cheaper than drama production, and the editor controls 100% of the pacing in post.

What we don't recommend (deliberately restrained)

  • No paid distribution at launch. YouTube monetisation requires 1k subs + 4k watch hours; we get there organically via the clip engine. Paid spend before we know which creative works wastes budget.
  • No multiple readers / no rotating cast. Jesse's earlier "five voices, one per disciple" idea is overridden by the pivot — one reader is the visual anchor, full stop. Re-introducing a rotating cast collapses the model back to drama.
  • No new Bear content commissioned for this engine. Bear is a patron. Two calendar-anchored posts/year on his own channels using existing archive footage — that's the entirety of his on-camera load.
  • No third-party podcast deals before the run is proving itself. Save the relationship trades for when we have an audience to bring.

Why this beats the original drama plan

  • Half the cost (~£26k vs the £35–45k year-one range the drama version implied once casting + scenes + costume were honest).
  • Single shoot, single reader. No casting fragility, no scheduling chains, no scenes that have to "match" between episodes.
  • YouTube-native. 8-minute format clears mid-roll monetisation; episode rhythm matches the platform's preference for sequenced playlists.
  • Bear's load is zero new filming, not the 3–4 set-pieces/year an earlier draft assumed. Removes the single biggest production risk.
  • The audiobook does the storytelling. We're amplifying an asset Hodder already produced and owns rights to — not building a new narrative product from scratch.
